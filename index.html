Creeping Red Fescue

Creeping Red Fiscueis an easy, low-maintenance, fine-bladed grass that is found almost everywhere. With a name likeredfescue, it almost seems like a misnomer. The "red" comes from the dark, reddish-color at the base of the stalk on each blade. In reality, this species presents itself with a rich, medium-to-dark green color, making for beautiful landscapes! As for the "creeping", that just describes how this tough plant grows and spreads throughout the soil. After earning a tough reputation of being able to thrive in all climates, including drought-like conditions, and being able to stand up to being trampled on, creeping red fescue is a favorite amongst landscapers for lawns, golf courses, fields, and playgrounds everywhere!

Kentucky Bluegrass

Named after what gives it it's charm, Kentucky Bluegrass offers a unique display of rich, emerald to blue-green colors. Despite the state of Kentucky laying claim to the nickname "Bluegrass State", this grass species did not originate from there. Native to Europe and northern Asia, this beautifully-colored species became popular in the U.S. as a pasture grass in states like Kentucky. Although this grass is more susceptible to drought-like conditions and may need some extra attention to maintain, the end result is worth the effort!

St. Augustine Grass

St. Augustine grass is reminiscent of the kind of grass you would have likely played in as a child; large-bladed, roughly cut, thick growth. St. Augustine grass is one of the most popular lawn grasses in the coastal state of Florida! Forming a thick, dense turf, this grass establishes itself quickly and has a remarkable trait - it has a high tolerance for salt! Making it an ideal choice for coastal lawns and yards! Thriving in environments with high humidity, it is also a popular grass in Australia. Also known as "buffalo turf" for it's dense, "carpet-like" sod, this grass is also commonly used in pastures and on ranches for livestock to graze on.
